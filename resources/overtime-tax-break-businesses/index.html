Overtime Tax Break: What It Means for Businesses

Overtime just got a tax break — but what does that really mean for employees and employers in 24/7 operations?

Legislation
Home›Resources›Overtime Tax Break
LegislationApril 20266 min read

With the passage of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) on July 4, 2025, overtime premiums are now partially shielded from federal income tax. Below we outline the details of the change and its impact on both employees and employers.

What Are the New Provisions?

As with many tax laws, the legislation includes provisions, caps, and definitions that matter. The key specifics:

Understanding "Qualified Overtime Compensation"

This definition is where most of the confusion lies. The deduction applies only to overtime required under Section 7 of the Fair Labor Standards Act that exceeds the regular rate of pay — specifically the 50% premium portion, the "half" in "time and a half."

✓ Qualifies for the deduction

What's Covered

  • The 50% premium on FLSA-required overtime
  • Non-exempt employees working more than 40 hours per week
  • Overtime required under federal FLSA — Section 7
✗ Does NOT qualify

What's Excluded

  • Daily overtime (after 8, 10, or any daily hour limit)
  • Automatic weekend or holiday premiums
  • Premiums above 50% such as double time
  • Time paid for vacation, sick leave, FMLA, or holidays
  • Employer-defined "overtime" broader than FLSA definition

What This Means for Employees

The most immediate benefit is a reduction in federal income tax on qualifying overtime earnings. For 2025, employees will not see the reduction in their paychecks directly — employers are maintaining current withholding this year. Workers will realize their savings when they file their 2025 federal income tax return.

The $12,500 cap affects fewer workers than headlines suggest. A worker earning $30 per hour has a $15 overtime premium. To reach the cap, they would need to work approximately 833 hours of overtime — more than 16 hours per week on average.

What This Means for Businesses

For businesses, the OBBBA creates both opportunity and administrative obligation. On the opportunity side, tax-advantaged overtime is a genuine recruiting and retention talking point for manufacturing and distribution operations competing for hourly workers. On the obligation side, employers are now required to report qualified overtime compensation as a separate line item on employees' W-2 forms.

For 2025, the IRS has provided transition relief — employers can use a reasonable estimation method. Starting in 2026, separate reporting will be required.

Two cautions for employers

The temptation to rely more heavily on overtime because it is now tax-advantaged for employees does not change your cost as an employer — you still pay time and a half. The long-term risks of high overtime — fatigue, quality issues, turnover — are entirely unaffected by what employees save on their taxes.

FLSA classification accuracy matters more now. The deduction applies only to non-exempt employees working FLSA-required overtime. Misclassification creates compliance exposure.

What Changed — and What Didn't

The overtime tax break is real and worth communicating to your workforce. It is not, however, a reason to restructure your staffing model around overtime. The economics of your operation have not changed. What has changed is that employees who work overtime have a modest but meaningful financial incentive to do so — which is useful context for recruiting, retention conversations, and overtime distribution policy.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the overtime tax break under the OBBBA?
The One Big Beautiful Bill Act created an above-the-line federal tax deduction on qualified overtime compensation, retroactive to January 1, 2025 and expiring December 31, 2028. The deduction covers up to $12,500 annually per individual ($25,000 for joint filers) and applies only to the 50% premium portion of FLSA-required overtime. It is available even to workers who take the standard deduction.
What counts as qualified overtime compensation under the OBBBA?
Only the 50% premium on overtime required under Section 7 of the Fair Labor Standards Act qualifies. Daily overtime triggered by state rules or company policy, automatic weekend or holiday premiums, double time, and time paid for vacation, sick leave, or holidays do not qualify. Many workplaces define overtime more broadly than the FLSA does — that more generous definition generally does not qualify for the deduction.
Does the overtime tax break reduce employer costs?
No. The deduction applies to the employee's federal income tax — not the employer's cost. Employers still pay time and a half for all overtime hours. FICA taxes still apply to all overtime earnings on both sides. The tax break changes what employees net from overtime, not what the employer pays.
Should employers increase overtime now that it is tax-advantaged for workers?
No. The long-term risks of high overtime — fatigue, quality issues, safety incidents, and turnover — are entirely unaffected by what employees save on their taxes. The employer's cost per overtime hour has not changed. The tax break is useful context for recruiting and retention conversations but does not change the operational economics of overtime versus hiring.
What new reporting requirements do employers have under the OBBBA?
Employers are now required to report qualified overtime compensation as a separate line item on employees' W-2 forms. For 2025, the IRS provided transition relief — employers can use a reasonable estimation method. Starting in 2026, separate reporting of qualifying overtime compensation is required. FLSA classification accuracy has become more important because misclassification creates compliance exposure.
