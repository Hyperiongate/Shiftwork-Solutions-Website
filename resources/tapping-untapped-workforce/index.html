Tapping Into the Untapped Workforce

Creative ways to expand your hiring pool in a tight labor market — through smarter scheduling and deliberate policy design.

Labor Markets
Home›Resources›Tapping Into the Untapped Workforce
TrendingApril 20265 min read

The labor shortage isn’t going away uniformly — but it is shifting. Across manufacturing and distribution, employers are facing the same core challenge: finding enough reliable people to keep operations running smoothly. Yet while companies compete for the same pool of traditional candidates, a wide range of capable workers remain on the sidelines.

These are individuals who could join the workforce if barriers were lowered or flexibility was offered — parents with childcare challenges, students, part-timers, and members of Gen Z rethinking their career paths. With thoughtful scheduling and policy adjustments, employers can unlock this hidden talent. Small changes — a summer swap program, a casual call-in list, a smarter approach to benefits — can broaden the hiring base and strengthen retention without requiring a complete operational overhaul.

Parents with Childcare Barriers

Parents who want to work but can’t find or afford daycare represent one of the largest untapped talent pools available to manufacturers and distribution centers. Traditional solutions — onsite daycare or direct subsidies — come with real drawbacks: the cost and complexity of running childcare services, or the perception of unfairness among employees without children.

More practical approaches thread that needle. Broadening the benefits package so that childcare support sits alongside wellness stipends or other perks makes it feel like part of a comprehensive offer rather than a program for a subset of employees. A “summer swap” arrangement — allowing parents more flexibility during summer months while filling those shifts with students looking for seasonal work — creates a genuine win on both sides without disrupting production coverage.

Practical Approach: A summer swap program pairs parents who need reduced summer hours with students seeking seasonal income — each group gets what they need, and coverage is maintained without adding headcount or cost.

Students, Part-Timers, and Flex-Seekers

Not everyone can or wants to commit to 40 hours a week. Students, people with second incomes, and those juggling personal commitments often seek flexible arrangements. They may not fit the traditional full-time profile, but they can be a valuable addition if the right structures are in place.

A Casual Call-In List is one of the most practical tools available here. Rather than relying solely on temp agencies, operations can maintain their own roster of people willing to work occasional shifts — providing a flexible staffing cushion while giving local workers extra income opportunities. The key is matching task complexity to worker experience: routine work goes to casual workers, while trained full-timers step into more complex roles.

The balance matters. Casual arrangements should strengthen the core workforce, not erode it by making part-time work so attractive that full-timers migrate toward reduced hours.

Gen Z and the Blue-Collar Shift

There is a genuine opportunity with younger workers that many operations are missing. While Gen Z is often characterized as difficult to recruit for industrial roles, a meaningful share of this generation is moving toward the trades — driven partly by concerns about AI displacing white-collar work, and partly by the appeal of immediate earning potential without significant debt.

Manufacturing plants and distribution centers that partner with trade schools and offer structured on-the-job training are already capturing this talent. Clear career pathways, competitive wages, and schedule predictability matter to this group. Fixed shifts with a visible path to preferred hours through seniority address what they’re actually looking for — which is stability, not unpredictability.

Summer Swap — pair parents needing summer flexibility with students seeking seasonal income. No new headcount. No coverage gap.
Casual Call-In List — your own roster of occasional workers. Reduces temp agency dependence and builds a known, reliable pool.
Trade School Partnerships — structured pathways into industrial roles with clear seniority and schedule progression for Gen Z workers.

Turning Ideas into Action

The strategies above are straightforward in concept and genuinely complex in execution. What’s the ROI of an expanded benefits package? How do you structure a summer swap without disrupting production flow? What pay policies support casual workers while maintaining fairness for your full-time core?

These are the questions where the details determine whether a good idea actually works. The operations that get this right don’t improvise — they design the flexibility deliberately, model the cost implications in advance, and build the policies before the need becomes urgent.
