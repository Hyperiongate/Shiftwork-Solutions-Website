When the Schedule Looks Fine on Paper

"We know the schedule isn't perfect, but..." That sentence is how a lot of avoidable problems stay in place for years.

Schedule Design
Home› Resources› When the Schedule Looks Fine on Paper
Schedule Design April 2026 6 min read

"We know the schedule isn't perfect, but the employees are used to it. And honestly, we've got bigger issues right now. Maybe in a year or two we'll revisit it."

We've heard some version of that sentence in a conference room — or on a plant floor walk — more times than we can count. It usually comes from someone who genuinely cares about the operation. Someone who isn't wrong that there are other priorities. Someone who believes they're making a reasonable judgment call about timing.

They're not wrong that change is disruptive. They are usually wrong about what "waiting" actually costs.

What "Fine" Actually Costs

Schedule problems don't hold steady while you're waiting for the right moment. They compound. And the compounding is largely invisible in any single month — which is precisely why it persists.

Consider a plant running 12-hour shifts on a 4-crew schedule. The schedule is nominally "fine" — it covers all 168 hours of the week, it's been in place for years, and people know how it works. But the overtime rate is running at 12%, when a well-designed schedule should require about 6% to cover normal absences and variations.

That extra 6% of overtime isn't dramatic on its own. Multiply it across a full year, across a full workforce, and it represents a significant labor cost that has no corresponding productivity gain. It's not covering an unusually difficult period. It's structural — baked into the schedule design itself.

12%
Overtime rate at a "fine" operation — roughly double what a well-designed schedule requires
28%
Annual turnover rate at operations with schedules employees describe as "hard to work around" — versus 14% at operations with well-designed schedules
Years
How long the compounding goes on when "we'll revisit it in a year or two" gets repeated at each year's planning cycle

Now add the turnover picture. Operations with schedules employees describe as "hard to work around" — inconsistent days off, frequent night rotation, unpredictable extra shifts — show annual turnover rates roughly twice as high as operations with schedules employees consider fair and predictable. Each turnover event carries recruiting, onboarding, and productivity loss costs. These aren't in the overtime line item, so they rarely get connected to the schedule in the post-mortem conversation.

And there's a subtler cost that almost never gets measured: the understaffed night shift. On many "fine" schedules, the night shift is chronically short — either because the schedule assigns fewer bodies to it by design, or because absences fall disproportionately on nights when supervisory presence is lowest. A workforce that's regularly running short on nights develops workarounds that become invisible load-bearing structures: the informal agreements between crews, the tasks quietly deferred, the quality checks that happen when time allows. None of this shows up as a line item. All of it is a cost.

The Rationalization Inventory

Every operation has its version of the reasons the schedule doesn't get fixed. They are almost always reasonable-sounding. They are almost always insufficient.

Rationalization 1

"The employees are used to it. Change would cause more problems than it solves."

The reality

Employees adapt to their current schedule, but "used to it" and "satisfied with it" are not the same thing. Workers who have organized their lives around a difficult schedule will resist change — but they are also leaving because of that schedule. Turnover is the silent vote against the status quo. A well-managed change process, with employee input built in, produces far less disruption than the steady attrition of people who find better options.

Rationalization 2

"We've got bigger issues right now. The schedule can wait."

The reality

The "bigger issues" are frequently downstream effects of the schedule. High overtime? Schedule design problem. Chronic understaffing on nights? Schedule design problem. Turnover concentrated in specific crews? Schedule design problem. Fixing the root cause addresses several "bigger issues" simultaneously. Addressing the symptoms individually while leaving the root cause in place is the definition of ongoing cost with no lasting resolution.

Rationalization 3

"We looked at this two years ago and it wasn't worth the disruption."

The reality

Two years ago, labor market conditions, workforce demographics, overtime costs, and the competitive landscape for hiring were different. The calculation that said "not worth it" in one environment may be wrong in the current one. More importantly, the disruption cost of a well-managed schedule change is a one-time event. The cost of the current schedule is recurring — it compounds every week. The math on "worth it" deserves a current look, not a two-year-old one.

Rationalization 4

"The union will never go for it."

The reality

Union leadership's position on schedule change is shaped by what they believe the membership wants — and that belief is often based on a vocal minority rather than a systematic understanding of the workforce's actual preferences. Operations that have used structured, anonymous employee input processes before approaching the union have consistently found that the workforce's preferences and the union's assumed position are not identical. Employee survey data changes the negotiating dynamic significantly. The assumption that the union won't go for it is often untested.

The cost of waiting isn't the cost of the change you're deferring. It's the cost of the compounding problem you're not addressing — accumulating quietly in overtime, turnover, and quality variance while the calendar runs.

At some point, the operation stops being able to afford to wait. That moment usually arrives not as a single dramatic event but as a slow-building recognition that the numbers that "weren't alarming" last year are hard to explain this year, and that the "year or two" conversation has been happening for longer than anyone remembers.
