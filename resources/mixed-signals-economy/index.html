The Mixed Signals Economy: What Shifting Labor Markets Mean for 24/7 Operations

Manufacturing is expanding. Employment is contracting. Unemployment is rising. What are operations leaders supposed to do with that?

Economy
Home›Resources›The Mixed Signals Economy
EconomyApril 20266 min read

The economic signals coming into 2026 don't tell a clean story. U.S. manufacturing output rose in February for the second consecutive month — only the third time in 40 months that the sector has expanded. Federal Reserve data puts output growth at 0.2% for the month. The ISM Manufacturing PMI confirmed the expansion, with 12 industries reporting growth.

That sounds like good news. And in some ways it is.

But look closer and the picture gets complicated. The ISM employment index was contracting in the same report. Total nonfarm payroll employment dropped by 92,000 in February. And price pressures — driven by tariffs, supply chain uncertainty, and volatile input costs — remain a significant headwind for manufacturers trying to plan more than a quarter ahead.

Expanding output. Shrinking employment. Rising unemployment. Persistent cost pressure. These signals aren't supposed to coexist, and yet here we are.

What's Actually Happening

The gap between output growth and employment contraction isn't a mystery — it's a pattern. When manufacturers are uncertain about whether demand will hold, they don't hire. They run existing crews harder. They extend overtime. They defer the headcount decision until the signal gets clearer.

The result is a workforce that's producing more with the same — or fewer — people. That's sustainable for a quarter. It isn't sustainable as a strategy.

At the same time, the labor market is softening in some places and remaining tight in others. Blue-collar workforce availability has been further complicated by policy-driven reductions in immigrant labor — a population that has historically filled a significant share of production, processing, and distribution roles. The national unemployment number ticking upward doesn't mean the worker you need is suddenly available in your region, in your industry, at your shift hours.

The simplest version of the story — "labor is scarce, compete harder" — no longer captures what's happening. But neither does "labor is loosening up, the pressure is off." The reality is more unstable than either narrative, and instability is its own kind of operational risk.

"The companies that consistently outperform through uncertain periods aren't the ones that predicted the future correctly. They're the ones that built workforce architectures flexible enough to respond in either direction."

The Freeze Instinct Is Costly

When signals are mixed, the instinct is to wait. Hold off on schedule changes. Defer the workforce strategy conversation. See how things shake out before committing to anything.

It's understandable. It's also expensive.

Operations that freeze workforce decisions during uncertainty don't hold steady — they drift. Overtime creeps up to cover coverage gaps that were supposed to be temporary. Turnover rises because employees read the instability and start looking. Schedules that were already straining become harder to defend. And when the picture does clarify — in either direction — the organization is less prepared to move than it would have been if it had kept building.

The companies that consistently outperform through uncertain periods aren't the ones that predicted the future correctly. They're the ones that built workforce architectures flexible enough to respond in either direction.

What Flexibility Actually Means

In a mixed-signals environment, workforce flexibility isn't a vague aspiration — it has specific, practical dimensions.

It means knowing how much of your current coverage depends on overtime versus scheduled headcount, and whether that ratio is a choice or an accident. Operations running 15% or 20% overtime as a structural baseline have almost no room to absorb a demand spike without burning out their workforce or blowing their labor budget.

It means understanding your workforce's actual overtime preferences — the roughly 20% who want all you can offer, the 20% who want none, and the 60% in the middle — and designing distribution policies around that reality rather than defaulting to equal distribution that satisfies almost no one.

It means having a schedule architecture that can scale headcount up or down without requiring a complete redesign. Manufacturing companies and distribution centers that built their staffing models for a specific demand level are the most exposed when that level shifts — in either direction.

And it means not waiting until the signal is clear to have these conversations, because by then the decisions will be reactive rather than considered.

The Honest Takeaway

Nobody knows exactly where this goes. Manufacturing momentum is real but fragile. Labor market softening is uneven. Cost pressures aren't resolving. Policy uncertainty adds a variable that no workforce model can fully absorb.

What that means for operations leaders isn't paralysis — it's the opposite. The right response to an unstable environment is a more deliberate workforce strategy, not a deferred one. The organizations that will be best positioned when the picture clarifies are the ones using this period to examine what their current schedule and staffing architecture can actually handle — and what it can't.

That's worth knowing now, regardless of what February's numbers look like in six months.
